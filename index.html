Dengue is not a rare tropical footnote here — it is an annual emergency. Over the past decade Malaysia logged more than 900,000 cases. The burden has never been steady: it climbs, it breaks records, and once — in 2021 — it collapsed to a fraction of its normal size.

Case numbers measure scale; deaths measure the human cost — and the two have pulled apart. Even as outbreaks rebounded toward record size, the share of patients who died kept falling. Better, faster treatment is the reason.

The timing

When does dengue strike?

Strip away the year-to-year drama and a steady heartbeat remains. Dengue has a season. Knowing its rhythm is how hospitals, councils and families prepare before the wave — not after.

3
The cause

Why that timing — and how much warning do we get?

Dengue outbreaks are not instant reactions to a storm. Rain leaves standing water; mosquitoes breed; the virus incubates. That chain takes time — which means the rain gives us a warning window before the cases arrive.

Rain first, cases later

Source: Open-Meteo Historical Weather API (ERA5) · OpenDengue

Line the two curves up and the gap becomes a number. Shift the case curve back by about eight weeks and it locks onto the rainfall curve — a roughly two-month fuse between a wet month and the outbreak it feeds.

4
The geography

Where is the risk highest?

Dengue does not spread evenly. It concentrates in dense, fast-growing states — and one state, Selangor, carries more than half the national burden.

Does a bigger population simply mean more cases? Not quite. Plotting each state’s infection rate against its size shows Selangor and Kuala Lumpur sitting well above the trend — high risk even after accounting for how many people live there — while the Borneo states sit below it.

Seen across the whole decade, the imbalance is structural. Selangor never drops below 45% of all national cases — a concentration that points to urban density and construction-site water, not weather, as the driver.

5
The response

Can we do anything about it?

If rain and rhythm decided everything, 2020–2021 should have been ordinary. Instead cases fell across every state at once. The reason wasn’t in the sky — it was in the streets. Movement-control orders kept people home, and dengue fell with them.

Weather sets the stage.
Human behaviour writes the script.

The ranking of the worst-hit states makes the volatility visible. Selangor never leaves the top spot — but the fight for second through fifth shifts constantly, which means an outbreak can flare almost anywhere.

There is one more reason outbreaks keep returning: the virus itself changes. Four dengue strains circulate, and when a new one takes over, people have little immunity and cases spike. DENV2 drove the 2015 epidemic; DENV1 now makes up about half of circulating strains — a warning for the next wave.

It rains. Weeks later, the hospitals fill up. But in 2021 the rule broke — proof that what we do matters as much as what the sky does. Remove standing water. Watch the season and the forecast. Act in the warning window, before the wave arrives.
